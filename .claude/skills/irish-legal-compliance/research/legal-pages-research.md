_Research completed 22 April 2026_

This document covers what is legally required on each legal/policy page for an Irish solicitor's website, sample compliance language, implementation timelines, checklists, and risk ratings.

## 1. Privacy Policy

### What requires it
- **GDPR Article 13 & 14** — mandatory transparency obligation whenever personal data is collected
- **Data Protection Act 2018** — Irish implementing legislation
- Triggered any time someone submits the contact form, or any analytics/tracking is active

### What must be included (Articles 13 & 14 GDPR)
1. Identity and contact details of the data controller (the solicitor)
2. Contact details of the Data Protection Officer (if appointed — small firms are generally not required to have one)
3. Purposes of processing and the lawful basis for each (e.g. legitimate interest, contract, consent)
4. If relying on legitimate interests — what those interests are
5. Any other recipients of the personal data (e.g. Resend for the contact form, Google Analytics)
6. Details of any transfers outside the EU/EEA, and what safeguards are in place
7. Data retention period (or the criteria used to determine it)
8. Right of access
9. Right to rectification
10. Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
11. Right to restrict processing
12. Right to data portability
13. Right to object
14. Right to withdraw consent (where processing is consent-based)
15. Right to lodge a complaint with the Data Protection Commission Ireland
16. Whether providing data is a legal or contractual requirement, and what happens if you don't
17. Whether automated decision-making or profiling takes place

### Solicitor-specific considerations
- **Client confidentiality vs. GDPR transparency** — GDPR requires you to tell people how their data is used; legal privilege means some of that information cannot be disclosed. The balance: be transparent about the *categories* of processing without disclosing privileged communications.
- **Anti-money laundering (AML)** — the Criminal Justice (Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing) Acts require retention of certain client identification data for a minimum of 5 years. This retention period must be documented in the privacy policy.
- **Court filings** — data shared with courts, the Land Registry, or Revenue is processed under statutory authority. Acknowledge this in the policy but note it falls outside the normal data subject rights framework.

### Sample compliance language

**General processing statement:**
> Mary Molloy Solicitor collects and processes personal data in connection with providing legal services to clients, responding to enquiries, and managing our website. We act as data controller in respect of personal data we hold. We process personal data on the lawful bases of contract performance, legal obligation, and legitimate interests. We do not sell personal data to third parties.

**AML data retention notice:**
> Under the Criminal Justice (Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing) Acts 2010–2021, we are required to verify the identity of clients and retain those records for a minimum of five years from the end of our business relationship. This is a legal obligation. During this period, the usual right to erasure does not apply to these records.

**Client confidentiality vs. transparency balance:**
> Where we process personal data in the course of providing legal services, some of that information is subject to legal professional privilege. We cannot disclose privileged communications even in response to a data subject access request. Where we withhold information on this basis, we will tell you that we have done so.

**Penalties for non-compliance**
- GDPR fines: up to **€20 million or 4% of global turnover**
- The DPC was responsible for enforcing more than half of the €1.2 billion in European data fines in 2024

- [DPC — Right to be informed (Articles 13 & 14)](https://www.dataprotection.ie/en/individuals/know-your-rights/right-be-informed-transparency-article-13-14-gdpr)
- [DPC — GDPR Checklist & Templates for SMEs (Word doc)](https://www.dataprotection.ie/sites/default/files/uploads/2019-04/GDPR-Checklist-Templates-for-SMEs-Downloadable-1.docx)

## 2. Cookie Policy

### What requires it
- **S.I. No. 336/2011** — Irish transposition of the EU ePrivacy Directive
- **GDPR** — sets the standard for what valid consent looks like
- Required on any site that sets cookies beyond strictly necessary ones

### What must be included
1. What cookies/tracking technologies the site uses
2. The purpose of each cookie
3. How long each cookie lasts
4. Whether any cookies are set by third parties (e.g. Google Analytics, Facebook Pixel)
5. Whether any data collected is transferred outside the EU
6. How the user can manage or withdraw consent
7. A mechanism for giving and withdrawing consent (a cookie banner — separate from the policy page but must link to it)

### Categories to disclose
- **Strictly necessary** — exempt from consent requirement
- **Functional / preference** — require consent
- **Analytics / performance** — require consent
- **Marketing / targeting** — require consent

### Ireland-specific rule
Cookie consent expires after **6 months** and must be refreshed — one of the strictest rules in Europe. Cookiebot handles this automatically.

### Prohibited consent practices
- Pre-checked consent boxes
- Cookie walls (forcing acceptance to access the site)
- Scroll-based consent
- Hidden or harder-to-find reject buttons (reject must be as easy as accept)

### Sample compliance language

**Cookie banner intro text:**
> We use cookies to improve your experience on this website. Some cookies are essential for the site to function; others help us understand how visitors use our site. You can accept all cookies, decline non-essential cookies, or manage your preferences below.

**Cookie policy opening statement:**
> This cookie policy explains what cookies are, which cookies this website uses, and how you can control them. It should be read alongside our Privacy Policy. This website is operated by Mary Molloy Solicitor, 2 Rose Inn Street, Kilkenny. By using this website you agree to our use of strictly necessary cookies. For all other cookies, we ask for your consent.

### Penalties for non-compliance
- Up to **€250,000** under S.I. No. 336/2011
- GDPR fines on top if personal data is involved

### Official sources
- [S.I. No. 336/2011 — full text](https://www.irishstatutebook.ie/eli/2011/si/336/)
- [DPC — What is the law on the use of cookies?](https://www.dataprotection.ie/en/faqs/cookies/what-law-use-cookies)
- [DPC — Guidance on Cookies and Other Tracking Technologies](https://www.dataprotection.ie/en/dpc-guidance/guidance-cookies-and-other-tracking-technologies)
- [DPC — Full guidance note on cookies (PDF)](https://www.dataprotection.ie/sites/default/files/uploads/2020-04/Guidance%20note%20on%20cookies%20and%20other%20tracking%20technologies.pdf)

### Tools / templates
- **Cookiebot** — [cookiebot.com](https://www.cookiebot.com/) — scans the site, auto-generates the policy, provides the consent banner, handles 6-month renewal. Free up to 100 pages. **Recommended.**
- **CookieYes** — similar tool, also widely used
- **OneTrust** — enterprise-level, more complex than needed for a small firm

## 3. Disclaimer

### What requires it
No specific Irish statute mandates a disclaimer. However it is **strongly recommended** because:
- S.I. No. 644/2020 prohibits misleading advertising — a disclaimer prevents website content being construed as specific legal advice
- Without one, a user could argue the website content created a solicitor-client relationship
- The Law Society uses disclaimers on its own website — this sets the professional standard

### What should be included
1. Website content is for general information only — not legal advice
2. No solicitor-client relationship is created by reading the website
3. The law described may not reflect the most current legal developments
4. Users should not act on website content without seeking specific legal advice
5. The firm accepts no liability for any reliance placed on the website content
6. Links to external sites are not endorsed by the firm
7. Professional qualifications and registration details (Law Society of Ireland)
8. Professional indemnity insurance held (as required by Law Society regulations)

### Sample compliance language

**Core disclaimer statement:**
> The information contained on this website is provided for general information purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. You should not act or refrain from acting on the basis of any content on this website without first obtaining specific legal advice from a qualified solicitor. No solicitor-client relationship is created by your use of this website or any communication through it.

**Professional registration disclosure:**
> Mary Molloy Solicitor is a solicitor's practice regulated by the Law Society of Ireland. Mary Molloy, Nicholas O'Shea, and Richard O'Shea are each admitted to the Roll of Solicitors maintained by the Law Society of Ireland. The firm holds professional indemnity insurance as required under the Solicitors Acts and regulations made thereunder.

**Professional indemnity insurance disclosure:**
> This firm maintains professional indemnity insurance in accordance with the requirements of the Law Society of Ireland. Details of the minimum terms and conditions of cover required by the Law Society are available from the Law Society of Ireland, Blackhall Place, Dublin 7.

**Conflict of interest protection language:**
> We are unable to act for a client where doing so would create a conflict of interest with an existing client or a former client. If a conflict arises during the course of a matter, we will advise you promptly and, where necessary, refer you to alternative representation.

### Official sources
- [Law Society of Ireland — Disclaimer on Find a Solicitor](https://www.lawsociety.ie/Find-a-Solicitor/Solicitor-Firm-Search/Disclaimer)
- [Law Society — Solicitor's Guide to Professional Conduct (PDF)](https://www.lawsociety.ie/globalassets/documents/committees/conduct-guide.pdf)

### Tools / templates
- No official template. Use the Law Society's own disclaimer (link above) as a reference. Must be written bespoke for the firm.

## 4. Terms of Use

### What requires it
- No single statute mandates terms of use for a solicitor website
- **EU Consumer Rights Directive** (transposed as S.I. No. 484/2013) — requires certain consumer information
- **eCommerce Directive** — requires online businesses to give users clear information
- Practically, terms of use protect the firm and set out acceptable use of the site

### What should be included
1. Who owns the website and its content (intellectual property)
2. Permitted use of the site content
3. That website content is not legal advice (cross-reference to disclaimer)
4. Limitation of liability for website errors or unavailability
5. Links to third-party sites — no endorsement, no responsibility
6. Governing law — Republic of Ireland, Irish courts
7. The firm's right to change website content at any time
8. Dispute resolution procedure

### Sample compliance language

**Opening statement:**
> These terms of use govern your access to and use of the website of Mary Molloy Solicitor at [domain]. By using this website you agree to these terms. If you do not agree, please do not use the website.

**Intellectual property clause:**
> All content on this website, including text, images, and design, is the property of Mary Molloy Solicitor or its licensors and is protected by Irish and EU copyright law. You may not reproduce, distribute, or republish any content without our prior written consent.

**Governing law clause:**
> These terms are governed by the laws of Ireland. Any dispute arising from your use of this website shall be subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the Irish courts.

### Official sources
- [LegalGuide.ie — Website Matters](https://legalguide.ie/website-matters/)
- [Law Society — Terms & Conditions on their own site](https://www.lawsociety.ie/About-this-Website/Terms--Conditions)

### Tools / templates
- [NetLawman.ie](https://www.netlawman.ie/dl/website-terms-conditions) — Irish-law T&C template (paid)

## 5. Accessibility Statement

### What requires it
- **S.I. No. 358/2020** applies to public sector bodies only — a private solicitor firm does not fall under this SI
- **S.I. No. 636/2023** transposes the EU Accessibility Act — extends to private sector service providers from **28 June 2025**. Legal services are covered.
- **Microenterprise exemption:** Firms with fewer than 10 employees and annual turnover/balance sheet under €2 million may be exempt. Mary Molloy Solicitor likely qualifies, but publishing a statement is still strongly recommended.
- Publishing a statement demonstrates good faith and protects against disability discrimination claims under the Equal Status Acts 2000–2015.

### What should be included (W3C / EU model format)
1. Name of the website and who it is for
2. Commitment to accessibility (WCAG 2.1 Level AA)
3. Compliance status — fully / partially / non-compliant (with reasons)
4. Known accessibility limitations
5. Technical specifications (browsers/assistive technologies tested)
6. Feedback mechanism — how users can report issues
7. Escalation — who to contact if response is unsatisfactory
8. Date the statement was last reviewed

### Sample compliance language

**Opening statement:**
> Mary Molloy Solicitor is committed to ensuring that this website is accessible to all users, including those with disabilities. We aim to meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA standard.

**Feedback mechanism:**
> If you experience any difficulty accessing content on this website, or if you require any information in an alternative format, please contact us by telephone at (056) 776 5829 or by post at 2 Rose Inn Street, Kilkenny. We will endeavour to respond to accessibility queries within 5 working days.

### Penalties (if EAA applies)
- Up to **€60,000 fine and/or 18 months imprisonment** for companies and directors

### Official sources
- [S.I. No. 358/2020 — full text](https://www.irishstatutebook.ie/eli/2020/si/358/made/en/print)
- [S.I. No. 636/2023 — EU Accessibility Act transposition](https://www.irishstatutebook.ie/eli/2023/si/636/made/en/print)
- [W3C WAI — Developing an Accessibility Statement](https://www.w3.org/WAI/planning/statements/)
- [gov.ie — Accessibility guidance](https://www.gov.ie/en/accessibility/)

### Tools / templates
- **W3C Accessibility Statement Generator** — [w3.org/WAI/planning/statements/generator/](https://www.w3.org/WAI/planning/statements/generator/) — free, official, nothing stored outside the browser. **Use this tool.**
- **WAVE** — [wave.webaim.org](https://wave.webaim.org/) — free browser tool to test the site before writing the statement
- **axe DevTools** — browser extension, more thorough than Lighthouse
- **Annertech** — Irish web accessibility specialists for a professional audit

## 6. Complaints Procedure

### What requires it
- **Legal Services Regulation Act 2015** — established the LSRA as the complaints body
- **Law Society Practice Note — Internal complaints procedure in solicitors' firms** — the standard all firms are expected to follow
- **S.I. No. 644/2020** — the advertising regulations framework under which the LSRA operates

### What must be included
1. That the firm has an internal complaints procedure
2. How to make a complaint (in writing to the principal — name and address)
3. Acknowledgement within **7 days**
4. Full written response within **14 days**
5. What remedies the firm can offer (apology, fee reduction, commitment to prevent recurrence)
6. The right to escalate to the LSRA if not satisfied
7. LSRA contact details ([lsra.ie](https://www.lsra.ie/))
8. The **3-year time limit** on LSRA complaints about inadequate services or excessive costs

### Sample compliance language

**Procedure opening:**
> Mary Molloy Solicitor is committed to providing a high standard of legal service. If you are dissatisfied with any aspect of our service, we want to hear from you. This page sets out how to make a complaint and what will happen next.

**How to complain:**
> Please set out your complaint in writing and address it to Mary Molloy, Principal, Mary Molloy Solicitor, 2 Rose Inn Street, Kilkenny, R95 W58D. You may also submit your complaint by email to [firm email address]. We will acknowledge receipt of your complaint within 7 working days and provide a full written response within 14 working days.

**LSRA escalation statement:**
> If you are not satisfied with our response to your complaint, you have the right to refer the matter to the Legal Services Regulatory Authority (LSRA). The LSRA is the independent body responsible for dealing with complaints against solicitors in Ireland. You can contact the LSRA at [lsra.ie](https://www.lsra.ie/) or by post at LSRA, PO Box 12906, Dublin 2. Please note that there is a three-year time limit on complaints to the LSRA about inadequate legal services or excessive costs, running from the date on which you became aware of the matter.

### Official sources
- [Law Society — Internal complaints procedure in solicitors' firms](https://www.lawsociety.ie/Solicitors/knowledge-base/Practice-Notes/Internal-complaints-procedure-in-solicitors-firms/)
- [Law Society — Complaints handling](https://www.lawsociety.ie/complaints-handling/)
- [Law Society — The complaints process](https://www.lawsociety.ie/complaints-handling/the-complaints-process/)
- [Law Society — Complaints against solicitors (public-facing)](https://www.lawsociety.ie/you-and-your-solicitor/complaints-against-solicitors/)
- [LSRA — lsra.ie](https://www.lsra.ie/)

### Tools / templates
- The Law Society Practice Note (link above) includes a template procedure — the closest thing to an official template. Must be adapted for the firm.
- No third-party generator exists for this page.
